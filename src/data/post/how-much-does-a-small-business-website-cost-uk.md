If you ask five companies to quote for a small business website, you’ll get five wildly different numbers.

One says **£300**. One says **£8,000**. One won’t give a price until you’ve sat through a “discovery call”.

That’s not because some are ripping you off and others aren’t. It’s because “a website” can mean almost anything — from a single page you build yourself in a weekend to a custom-coded system that takes months.

This guide breaks down what a small business website actually costs in the UK, what you’re really paying for, and how to make sure you don’t overpay (or underpay and have to redo it a year later).

---

## The short answer

For a typical UK small business — a tradesperson, salon, restaurant, clinic or local service — here are realistic 2020s price brackets:

- **DIY website builder:** £10–£30 per month (plus your own time)
- **Freelancer:** £500–£3,000 one-off
- **Small studio / agency (fixed price):** £1,500–£6,000 one-off
- **Larger agency or custom build:** £8,000–£25,000+

Most small businesses that want something professional — but not enterprise-grade — land somewhere in the **£1,500–£5,000** range for a well-built site that brings in enquiries.

Below that, you’re usually doing the work yourself. Above it, you’re paying for complexity most small businesses don’t need.

---

## What you’re actually paying for

The price isn’t really about “how many pages”. It’s about how much skilled work goes into it. The big drivers are:

### 1) Design
A template you fill in yourself is cheap. A design built around *your* business — your services, your photos, your customers’ questions — takes a designer’s time. That’s most of the cost gap between a £300 site and a £3,000 one.

### 2) Build quality
Two sites can look identical and perform completely differently. Speed, mobile layout, clean code and proper structure are invisible until something goes wrong — a slow site quietly loses you enquiries every single day.

### 3) Conversion, not just looks
A pretty site that doesn’t generate calls is an expensive business card. The work that turns visitors into enquiries — clear headlines, obvious calls-to-action, trust signals, fast quote forms — is where good money is well spent.

### 4) Content and SEO basics
Someone has to write the words, set up page titles and descriptions, and make sure Google can find you. If a quote is suspiciously cheap, this is usually the bit that’s been skipped.

## The three ways to get a website (and what each really costs)

### Option 1 — Do it yourself (Wix, Squarespace, GoDaddy)
**Cost:** ~£10–£30/month.

DIY builders are genuinely good now, and for a brand-new business on a tight budget they can be the right call.

The real cost is **your time** — and the result usually looks like a template, because it is one. If your evenings are worth more than the saving, this gets expensive fast.

### Option 2 — Hire a freelancer
**Cost:** £500–£3,000.

A good freelancer can build an excellent small business site. The trade-offs are availability and accountability — one person juggling several clients, who may be hard to reach when you need a change six months later.

Quality varies enormously at this level, so ask to see live sites they’ve built and check they still load fast today.

### Option 3 — A studio or agency
**Cost:** £1,500–£25,000+, depending heavily on the agency.

This is the widest bracket. A small, focused studio can deliver a professional, conversion-ready site for a few thousand pounds. A larger agency with account managers and a longer process will charge multiples of that for similar output — you’re often paying for their overheads, not your results.

The key question isn’t “agency or freelancer?” — it’s **“what am I actually getting for this number?”**

## The costs nobody mentions upfront

The build price is only part of the picture. Budget for these too:

- **Domain name:** ~£10–£15 per year
- **Hosting:** £0–£30 per month (many modern sites host for free or near-free)
- **SSL certificate:** usually free now — never pay extra for this
- **Maintenance / updates:** optional, but plan for occasional changes
- **Email:** business email (e.g. you@yourbusiness.co.uk) is usually a few pounds a month

Watch out for quotes that look cheap upfront but lock you into expensive monthly “maintenance” you can’t leave. Always ask: *do I own the site, and can I take it elsewhere?*

## So what should *you* spend?

A simple way to decide:

- **Brand new, testing an idea, near-zero budget?** Start with a DIY builder. Upgrade once you’re getting customers.
- **Established and relying on word-of-mouth, but losing work to better-looking competitors?** A professional fixed-price site in the £1,500–£5,000 range usually pays for itself in a handful of jobs.
- **Complex needs — bookings, e-commerce, multiple locations?** Budget more, and choose a builder who can show you similar work.

The wrong move is paying £300 for something you’ll have to rebuild in a year, or £15,000 for features you’ll never use. The right spend is the one that **brings in more enquiries than it costs** — and keeps doing it.
